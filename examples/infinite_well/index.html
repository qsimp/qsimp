What is this chart?

The above chart is the wave-function solution to the Schrödinger Equation for a particle stuck in a well/hole. The chart shows both the real and imaginary components for the first 5 stationary states given by :

$$ \Psi (x, t) = \sum_{n=1}^{5} \sqrt{ \frac{2}{a}} \sin{(\frac{n \pi}{a} x )} e^{-i (n^2 \pi^2 \hbar) / (2 m a^2 ) t} $$
  • \( \Psi \) is the wavefunction
  • \( n \) is the specific stationary states
  • \( a \) is the width of the well
  • \( x \) is the "position" of the wave within the well
  • \( i \) is an imaginary i (complex number)
  • \( \hbar \) is plank's constant (over 2pi)
  • \( m \) is the mass of the electron

Why is the chart so complex?

The chart looks complex because the electron is in a superposition of different possible states (this is common until the particle is measured). Each pair of waves corresponds to one of the states that the particle can be in, both the real and imagine component. One of the big insights of quantum mechanics is that when a particle is stuck in a well such as this, it's only allow to have specific amounts of energy.

But the particle can also be in a super position of those multiple states at the same time. It's not until the particle is measured that it falls into a particular state.

Probability State

In the above example, all the states have the same probability of occurring, but normally there's a coefficent in front that helps determine the probability of measuring a particle in a particular state. The probability of finding a particle in a particular state is \( |c_n|^2 \):

$$ \Psi (x, t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} c_n \sqrt{ \frac{2}{a}} \sin{(\frac{n \pi}{a} x )} e^{-i (n^2 \pi^2 \hbar) / (2 m a^2 ) t} $$ The different states and energies of our default example wave function ( \( a=10nm \) ) are:

Expectation Values

It's also possible calculate "expectation values" for different properties. These properties could be position \( x \), momentum \( p \) (which is a proxy for velocity.) From those two, you can calculate other quantities such as kinetic energy.

It's important to remember that the expectation value is the expected value if you were to measure a bunch of similar particles in a similar configuration.
